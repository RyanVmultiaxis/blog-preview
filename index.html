Don’t Fear AI. Leverage It.

By Ryan • Multiaxis Intelligence

AI has become one of those topics that instantly divides the room.

Some people are excited. Some are curious. Some are quietly using it every day and not talking about it. And then there are two groups that stand out the most: the people who are afraid it is going to take their job, and the people who are openly anti-AI.

The surprising part is how often that second group is made up of younger professionals. People in their 20s and 30s. People who are smart, talented, and already established in their careers.

I had a conversation recently with a professional in their late 20s who told me they had not really used AI because they “don’t like what it’s doing to my industry.”

I understand the sentiment. I really do. But it also highlights something we need to talk about honestly: whether you like it or not, this is happening. And the best move is not to fight it. It is to learn how to use it responsibly and use it as leverage.

The Fear Is Not New

If you have been around manufacturing long enough, you have seen this pattern play out over and over again.

When CAD replaced drafting tables, some people refused to touch it. When CNC began replacing manual machining, there were machinists who swore a program would never beat a handwheel. When spreadsheets and automation hit business workflows, people worried it would destroy entire professions.

What actually happened was simpler. The people who adapted became more valuable. The people who did not slowly became less relevant.

AI is not a new story. It is the same story, just louder.

AI Does Not Replace Skill. It Amplifies It.

Here is the part most people miss. AI does not create expertise. It does not replace experience. It does not magically make someone good at something they do not understand.

What it does do is remove friction.

A capable person using AI can move faster, explore more options, reduce repetitive work, communicate more clearly, and get to better decisions sooner. The craft still matters. Judgment still matters. Taste still matters. Domain knowledge still matters.

AI does not replace the person. It amplifies the person.

A Simple Analogy

Let us pull this out of the tech world for a second.

Say you need repairs done on your house, and you need them done quickly. You have two contractors:

  • An old-school carpenter who refuses to use power tools and insists on hammering in every nail by hand.
  • An equally skilled carpenter who uses modern tools, nail guns, laser levels, cordless saws, and finishes the job faster with the same craftsmanship.

Neither contractor lacks skill. The difference is leverage.

AI is the power tool.

Anti-AI Is Usually Anti-Change

When someone says they are anti-AI, what they usually mean is one of these:

  • They have seen it used in a lazy way, and they do not respect the output.
  • They are worried it will reduce the value of their work.
  • They do not want to be forced to learn something new.
  • They are frustrated by how fast it is moving.

Those are fair concerns. AI can absolutely be misused. It can flood industries with low-effort content. It can encourage shortcuts. It can be weaponized by people who do not care about quality.

But that does not change the direction of the world. It changes the responsibility of the professionals inside it.

AI is not the threat. The threat is pretending it is not happening.

The Stigma Is Real, and It Needs to Go Away

Two years ago, I was talking with a high-level professional in their mid-50s. They told me they had to put together a presentation for their boss that they were nervous about. They said, “I failed English class in school and I am bad at it.”

So I showed them a simple way to use AI. Not to invent ideas, and not to fake expertise. Just to re-word their own thoughts in a more professional way so the message was clear.

At the time, we were in a machine shop and I was talking loudly. They stopped me and said, “Keep it down. My boss is right over there.”

They were not worried about the content. The content was theirs. They were worried about the stigma. They were worried their boss would not accept the work if it had been re-worded using AI.

That is the part that needs to change.

Using AI for Communication Is a Smart Move

If you are communicating with customers, leadership, or anyone where spelling, punctuation, and clarity really matter, you should be using tools that help you communicate better.

That does not mean you blindly trust the output. You still proofread it. You still own the message. You still make sure it reflects your intent.

But this idea that you are somehow cheating because a tool helped you write more clearly is outdated. It is like refusing to use spellcheck. It is like refusing to use a calculator. It is like refusing to use power tools because you want to prove you can do it the hard way.

Professional communication is a skill. And for a lot of people, it is the difference between being understood and being overlooked.

The Real Risk: Being Outpaced

Here is the uncomfortable truth. AI adoption is not going to be optional for most industries long-term.

This does not mean your job disappears overnight. It means the baseline shifts. It means speed and efficiency become expected. It means output increases. And it means people who leverage AI will often be able to do more in less time while maintaining quality.

When two people are equally talented, the one who can research faster, iterate faster, communicate clearer, and respond quicker tends to get the opportunity.

Not because they replaced someone. Because they kept up.

What AI Is Actually Great For

AI shines in the areas most people do not love anyway. The busywork and the friction.

  • Turning scattered notes into clean structure
  • Summarizing long documents
  • Generating first drafts to react to
  • Brainstorming alternatives when you are stuck
  • Helping you communicate clearer and faster
  • Reducing blank page time

None of that removes the need for a skilled professional. It simply frees them up to spend more time where their value is highest.

Looking Ahead

AI is not here to take your job. But it is absolutely changing how work gets done.

The people who thrive will not be the loudest advocates or the harshest critics. They will be the ones who quietly integrate these tools into their workflow, improve their efficiency, raise the quality of their output, and keep their standards high.

Just like every major shift before it, this is not about replacement.

It is about leverage.
